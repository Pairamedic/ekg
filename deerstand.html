Localization

Arkansas Context: Terrain, Weather, & Response

Terrain & Access

  • Ozarks & Ouachitas: steep hollows, dense forest, rocky footing.
  • Delta floodplains: mud, bayous, levees, limited road access.
  • Expect limited radio/cell coverage—plan relay or alt comms.

Operations

  • Rural response & long transports to Level I/II trauma centers.
  • Mutual aid: AGFC, county VFDs, SAR teams, forestry units.
  • Stage safely for wildlife/weapon risks during hunting season.

Documentation Focus

  • Mechanism & environment (height, surface, weather, terrain).
  • Time down, access route/extrication method, comms issues.
  • Wildlife exposure, plants/insects, waterbody name if relevant.

Climbing Risks

Deer Stands & Ascents: Where Things Go Wrong

Common Causes

  • Wet/frosted steps, aged rungs, equipment snags.
  • No harness or incorrect tethering while climbing.
  • Fatigue or impairment during early AM/late PM ascents.

Scene Considerations

  • Estimate height & fall path (branches, rocks, stumps).
  • Steep terrain/mud; plan ATV/UTV access for carries.
  • Hypothermia during prolonged discovery/extrication.

Expected Injuries

  • Spine, pelvis, long-bone fractures; rib & thoracic injury.
  • TBI from ground/branch impact; occult hemorrhage.
Documentation Focus: Fall height, landing surface, position found, time down, harness presence/condition, extrication method.

Wildlife

Venomous Snakes in Arkansas

Pit Vipers

  • Copperhead — common in leaf litter; active into fall.
  • Cottonmouth — creeks/ponds/bayous; defensive near water.
  • Timber Rattlesnake — forested hills; significant envenomation.
  • Western Pygmy Rattlesnake — smaller, camouflaged; localized bites.

Elapids

  • Texas Coral Snake — rare in far SW Arkansas; neurotoxic.

Identification uncertain? Treat as venomous and avoid capture attempts.

Care & Coordination

  • Immobilize limb, remove jewelry, keep at heart level.
  • No ice, incision/suction, or tourniquet.
  • Our service does not carry antivenom — coordinate early with TraumaCom/receiving ER to confirm availability and destination.
Documentation Focus: Bite site & side, time of bite, species (if known), serial swelling measurements (q15), pain progression, waterbody/trail name if relevant.

Wildlife

Black Bears: Encounters & Attacks

Behavior & Triggers

  • Generally avoid humans; aggression is defensive.
  • Triggers: startled at close range, cub protection, food sources.
  • Activity peaks dawn/dusk; dense brush lowers visibility.

Scene Safety

  • Stage until law enforcement/AGFC confirm area secure.
  • Beware return to carcass/bait; keep egress path clear.
  • Unify crew; avoid cornering or separating responders.

Injury Patterns

  • Deep lacerations/avulsions (scalp/face/forearms).
  • Crush injuries, fractures, penetrating tooth wounds.
  • Hemorrhage & contamination → early prophylaxis per guidance.
Documentation Focus: Scene security confirmation (AGFC/LE), wound locations, hemorrhage control steps, analgesia, tetanus status, destination trauma capability.

Allergens

Closed Stands = Surprise Tenants (Wasps & Hornets)

Exposure

  • Nests inside unused stands/blinds, under seats/roofs.
  • Disturbance during entry → multiple stings quickly.

Clinical Focus

  • Watch for rapid progression to anaphylaxis (airway, wheeze, hypotension).
  • Epinephrine IM first-line; repeat per protocol; adjuncts PRN.

Team Safety

  • Identify nests before entry; have exit plan; relocate patient if swarming.
  • Monitor crew/bystanders—secondary anaphylaxis can occur.
Documentation Focus: Suspected source, onset time, number of stings, Epi timing/dose/response, airway findings, need for second dose.
Insects
Dermatologic

Poison Ivy, Oak, and Sumac

Exposure

  • Urushiol on plants, clothing, tools, pets; vines on stands.
  • Secondary transfer from contaminated gear is common.

Assessment

  • Pruritic vesicular rash, linear streaks, swelling.
  • Face/airway involvement = higher concern.

Management

  • Soap/water wash ASAP; avoid hot water early.
  • Topical/systemic therapy per protocol based on severity.
Documentation Focus: Exposure source/location, decontamination steps, spread/airway involvement, response to meds.

Mechanism

ATV / 4-Wheel Incidents

Common Mechanisms

  • Rollovers on slopes, sudden turns, hidden ruts.
  • Impairment; night riding without adequate lighting.

Injuries

  • Head/face trauma; C-spine injuries.
  • Long-bone fractures, crush/compartment concerns.
  • Thoracoabdominal injury from handlebar impact.

Scene Safety

  • Secondary vehicles, hunters, firearms in proximity.
  • Fuel leaks, hot engines, entanglement hazards.
Documentation Focus: Helmet use, speed/terrain, ejection/rollover, LOC, extrication, associated wildlife/weapons on scene.

Water

Dive & Shallow-Water Trauma (Local Waters)

Local Examples

  • Lake Pine Bluff & Lake Langhofer — submerged logs/equipment, sudden drop-offs.
  • Arkansas River — currents, low visibility, channel edges.
  • Bayou Bartholomew — dense vegetation, snags, variable depth.

Injury Patterns

  • C-spine fracture/dislocation (C5–C7 common), head lacerations.
  • LOC, aspiration, drowning/near-drowning, hypothermia.

Management Priorities

  • Airway/ventilation first; spinal precautions.
  • Warm, oxygenate, monitor; rapid transport to capability.
Documentation Focus: Waterbody name, entry point, depth estimate, visibility/current, timeline, bystander rescue details.

Arkansas Field Resources & Local Areas

Agencies & Contacts

  • AGFC — Arkansas Game & Fish Commission (wildlife, boat patrol, access)
  • TraumaCom / Receiving ER — confirm antivenom availability for snakebites
  • County VFDs & SAR teams — backwoods access/extrication
  • Arkansas Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222

Local Areas (Jefferson County)

  • Arkansas River, Lake Pine Bluff, Lake Langhofer
  • Bayou Bartholomew (slow water, vegetation/snags)
  • Byrd Lake Natural Area (trails, mixed forest)
  • Delta Rivers Nature Center (bottomland hardwood habitat)

Use these names in reports to improve clarity and interagency coordination.
