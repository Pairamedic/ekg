Climbing Risks

Deer Stands & Ascents: Where Things Go Wrong

Common Causes

  • Slips on wet/frosted steps; aged or loose rungs.
  • No harness or incorrect tethering while climbing.
  • Equipment snag (rifle/bow/pack) causing loss of balance.

Scene Considerations

  • Height estimate, fall path (branches, rocks, stumps).
  • Access route for extrication (steep terrain, mud).
  • Hypothermia risk during prolonged discovery.

Expected Injuries

  • Spine, pelvis, long‑bone fractures.
  • Rib fractures, pneumo/hemothorax, solid‑organ injury.
  • TBI from ground/branch impact.
Field tip: Maintain spinal precautions until low‑risk; watch for occult hemorrhage and exposure injuries.

Wildlife

Wildlife Encounters: Black Bears

Behavior & Triggers

  • Usually avoid humans; aggression often defensive.
  • Common triggers: startled at close range, cub protection, food sources (coolers, bait, carcasses).
  • Dawn/dusk activity; dense brush reduces visibility.

Scene Safety

  • Stage until law enforcement/game warden confirms area secure.
  • Beware of return to food sources; keep egress path clear.
  • Use loud verbal cues, avoid cornering; keep team together.

Injury Patterns

  • Deep lacerations/avulsions (scalp/face/forearms).
  • Crush injuries, fractures, penetrating tooth wounds.
  • Hemorrhage & contamination → early antibiotics per local guidance.
Priorities: Hemorrhage control (tourniquet/pressure dressing), airway protection, rapid analgesia, broad documentation of wound locations, and early notification of receiving trauma center.

Allergens

Closed Stands = Surprise Tenants

Wasp & Hornet Exposure

  • Nests inside unused stands/blinds, under seats or roofs.
  • Disturbance during entry can cause multiple stings rapidly.

Clinical Focus

  • Watch for rapid progression from local swelling to anaphylaxis.
  • Epinephrine IM first-line; repeat per protocol if symptoms persist.
  • Supportive: airway/oxygen, bronchodilator for wheeze, fluids for hypotension.

Team Safety

  • Approach cautiously; identify nests before entry.
  • Have a quick exit plan; consider relocating the patient before treatment if swarming continues.
  • Monitor crew/bystanders—secondary anaphylaxis can occur.

Tip: Stock epinephrine auto-injectors in fall kits; verify expiration dates before season.

Dermatologic

Poison Ivy, Oak, and Sumac

Exposure

  • Urushiol oil on plants, clothing, tools, pets.
  • Leaves may persist on vines/climbers around stands.

Assessment

  • Pruritic vesicular rash, linear streaks, swelling.
  • Face/airway involvement = higher concern.

Management

  • Soap/water wash ASAP; avoid hot water early.
  • Topical steroids/antihistamines per protocol; consider systemic if severe.

Mechanism

ATV / 4‑Wheel Incidents

Common Mechanisms

  • Rollovers on slopes, sudden turns, hidden ruts.
  • Operator impairment; night riding without lighting.

Injuries

  • Head/face trauma; cervical spine injuries.
  • Long‑bone fractures, crush/compartment concerns.
  • Thoracoabdominal injury from handlebar impact.

Scene Safety

  • Secondary vehicles, hunters, firearms in proximity.
  • Fuel leaks, hot engines, entanglement hazards.

Water

Dive & Shallow‑Water Trauma

Mechanisms

  • Dive into unknown depth; jump from dock/rocks.
  • Slip/fall into water with head impact.

Injury Patterns

  • C‑spine fracture/dislocation (C5–C7 common).
  • LOC, aspiration, drowning/near‑drowning.
  • Hypothermia during prolonged extrication.

Management Priorities

  • Airway/ventilation first; spinal precautions.
  • Warm, oxygenate, monitor; rapid transport to capability.
