EMS Documentation
Why it matters

Good documentation is clinical care, legal armor, and QA fuel.

  • Continuity: ED, hospitalists, coders rely on your story to carry care forward.
  • Medical‑legal: “If it isn’t documented, it didn’t happen.” Your documentation is your memory.
  • Quality & billing: Accurate elements = appropriate reimbursement & metrics.
  • Professionalism: Clear writing reflects clear thinking under stress.
Impact
Anatomy of a PCR

What must be present—every time

  • Dispatch & scene context (who called, where, safety, environment).
  • Initial impression & chief complaint in patient’s words.
  • Assessment: pertinent positives/negatives, vitals trends, pain scale.
  • Treatments: what, when, dose/route, response, complications.
  • Medical decision making: why you did or didn’t do something.
  • Disposition: handoff, receiving staff, condition on arrival, report given.

Pro tips

  • Use time stamps for key events; trends beat single data points.
  • Document informed refusals with capacity, risks explained, alternatives.
  • Redact identifiers in teaching examples; protect PHI.
  • Write for a smart non‑EMS reader (juror, nurse, coder).
Anatomy
Structure

Pick a framework and be consistent

SOAP

  • Subjective: patient story & chief complaint.
  • Objective: exam, vitals, monitor findings.
  • Assessment: differential & working impression.
  • Plan: interventions, transport, response.

CHART

  • Chief complaint
  • History (SAMPLE/OPQRST)
  • Assessment
  • Rx (treatments)
  • Transport (and handoff)

For refusals

  • Capacity & orientation documented
  • Risks/benefits explained, teach‑back noted
  • Alternatives offered & return precautions
  • Signatures/witness, vital signs, attempt to contact MD

Examples

Bad vs. better narratives

“Pt found on couch. Vitals taken. 324 ASA given. Transported to ER.”

  • No complaint, no exam, no decision making.
  • Medication lacks why, time, route, response.

“Dispatched 03:14 for 58M chest pain. On arrival 03:21, pt pale/diaphoretic, c/o substernal pressure 7/10 x35 min at rest; denies pleuritic pain. PMH HTN, hyperlipidemia; meds lisinopril, atorvastatin. Vitals 03:24: 152/92, 104, 22, SpO₂ 95% RA. ECG: 1mm ST dep V5‑V6, no elevation. Given 324mg ASA PO 03:26; pain to 5/10. Nitro withheld due to SBP trending 102 at 03:29. Oxygen titrated to 2 L NC → SpO₂ 98%. Transported code 2; report to RN Smith 03:47.”

Examples
Avoid

Common pitfalls

  • Copy‑paste from prior calls; mismatched genders/ages/complaints.
  • Vitals without times; single set of vitals for long transports.
  • “Patient tolerated well” with no response metrics.
  • Jargon/abbreviations without context; local slang.
  • Missing negatives that matter (e.g., no SOB, no neuro deficit).
Pitfalls
Defensibility

Write like future‑you is on the stand

  • Objective tone; avoid sarcasm or blame. Quote patients verbatim when relevant.
  • Time‑order events. Explain deviations from protocol or med control orders.
  • For high‑risk charts (peds, psych, refusals), include capacity and safety plan.
  • Document who you handed off to and that questions were answered.
Legal
Practice

Scenario sprints (3–5 minutes each)

  1. Medical refusal: 42F anxiety attack now resolved; wants to stay home. Build a refusal narrative.
  2. Trauma: MVC low‑speed, airbag deployed, no LOC. What negs must you include and why?
  3. Cardiac: ACS rule‑in vs rule‑out language; document reasoning for/against nitro.

Tip: have learners write on paper or in your ePCR training mode; then do quick peer review.

Practice
At the truck door

30‑second documentation checklist

  • Chief complaint in quotes
  • Pertinent positives & negatives
  • Vitals with times & trends
  • Decision‑making & rationale
  • Responses to treatment
  • Handoff details (to whom, when)
